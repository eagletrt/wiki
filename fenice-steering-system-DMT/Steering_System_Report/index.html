    FENICE STEERING SYSTEM

    Introduction

    The aim of this study is to calculate the Moment that the driver has to apply for steering the wheel given a Resisting torque at the two front wheels. In order to do so, a simplified 2D kinematic study was set up; here are the hypotheses to consider: - Only the resisting torque is considered as an external general force, so the forces that generate this moment are not taken into account. - The rotational axis of the front wheel is not considered to be inclined, but vertical and overlapping the intersection point between the plane through the center of the wheel and the actual rotational axis of the wheels. - Also, the steering arm was not considered to be inclined, but perfectly parallel to the XY plane. - Furthermore, the cardan joint between the steering wheel and the steering column, which would introduce sinusoidal distortion in the velocity ratio, is not considered in either kinematics or dynamics. - Finally it is not considered any camber angle or convergence angle of the wheel as well as the suspension escursion.

    Data

    Data refers to “FENICE EVO.iam” and are all expressed in mm for lengths and radians for angles. The measurements of the various components of the steering were taken from CAD. Below are images of how and where the measurements were taken. - L1 represents the rack length.

    • The steering arm, in the maple file is called L2.

    • The distance between the end of the steering arm and point D, in the maple file, is called L3. Since the kinematic study was conducted in 2D, it corresponds to the vector sum of the X and Y components.

    • The angle value between L3 and wheel axis, in the maple file is alfa.

    • tt is the car wheelbase.

    • The distance between the centre of the wheel and the rack, in the maple file is called s.

    • The images below represent the offset of point D on the x-y plane from the centre of the wheel.

    Kinematics

    A Recursive Approach was used to describe the motion of the system; the first point to take into consideration is the half-length of the rack PA and for us this is a fixed point while PB is fixed to the right end of the rack. The second point C was calculated by applying a first rotation of theta2(t) and a traslation of L2 (BC). The third point, as before, was calculated by applying a translation from the ground frame in order to reach the point D in the figure and a rotation of theta3(t). Finally, since the kinematic chain must be closed, the point PCa1 is calculated by rotating the reference frame with the offset angle 'alpha', then with a traslation of L3 it is finally possible to overlap the origin of the frame RF2dx and RF3a1. In this way, the right side of the mechanism is complete, and the variables are ready to be calculated as a function of the displacement q(t), which coincides with the position in the x-coordinate of the rack. Notice that this one must be represented as function of the steering wheel angle defined as theta1(t). The steering wheel angular motion is proportional to the rack linear displacement it is enough to put the reduction ratio in front of theta1(t).

    \[ q(t) = K*\theta_{1}(t) \]

    Where K is equal to

    \[ 108.38494654884787/(2 \pi ) \]

    From the definition of the reference frame, the angles are already defined in absolute frame and that allows to define the reference frame of the wheel as a pure translation from the centre of rotation of the wheel hub by offset and offsety. The approach to calculate the left end of the mechanism is the same as before the only difference is that the entire mechanism is mirrored respect to the y axis in figure. The dependent variable are theta4(t)(angle of the steering arm) and theta5(t) (rotation of the left wheel) obviously respond to the rack linear displacement defined in the first point of the right end of the mechanism of controlled by the rotation of the steering wheel.

    Velocity

    In this section it is computed the velocity of the the dependent variables to generate the necessary components to apply the principle of virtual work.

    DYNAMICS

    For the sake of completeness, the bodies are defined but not considered in the calculation of the moment in the next step. Definition of the bodies: - the steering shaft, - 2 steering arms, - the rack, - the 2 wheels hub, but not the wheels.

    PVW to calc Driver Torque

    First, define the general force acting on the whole system; in this case M(t) is the torque the driver has to apply, while the resisting torque is assumed to be 60 Nm.

    \[ \begin{gathered} Driver Torque=M(t); \\ Resisting Torque DX=60000; \\ Resisting Torque SX=60000; \\ \end{gathered} \]

    To set up the PVW is sufficient to balance the resisting torque with the driver torque multiplied by the delta theta of the wheels and the steering wheel.

    \[ M(t) \delta\theta_{1}(t) + ResTdx \delta\theta_{3}(t) + ResTsx \delta\theta_{5}(t) \]

    Now, by substituting the velocity of the dependent coordinates as a function of the independent ones, it is possible to evaluate the Moment the driver has to apply to counterbalance the resisting Torques.

    Finally, it is possible to plot the result as a function of the steering wheel rotation as well as the wheel rotation by substituting the kinematic solution calculated in the first point.

    PVW to calc Forces actinfg on the rack

    In order to verify the rack, it is computed the Force acting on that one. First it necessary to generate the Forces (Fx3, Fx5) which are applied at point B and E and aligned with the respectively steering arm. Apply the principal of virtual work for the Forces generated by the rack and the same Resisting Torques. The PVW is applied first for the right end of the mechanism and then for the left end, in this way we balance the work done by Fx3 with the one done with the Resisting Torque at the right wheel. So, we have the 2-equation needed to compute Fx3 and Fx5. The resolution is the same, so once solved the equation obtained by the PVW for Fx3 and Fx5; substitute first the velocity of the dependent variable as function of the independent and then substitute the kinematic solution. In this way it is possible to plot the Forces as function of the independent variables.

    Below the maximum value obtained will be used to verify the cross section of the rack in the weakest point: - F3x = 777.2436090 N - F3y = 43.35318373 N - F5x = 605.8957510 N - F5y = -61.31197008 N
